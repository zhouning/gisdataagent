\begin{letter}{The Editor-in-Chief \\ \emph{International Journal of Geographical Information Science}}

\opening{Dear Editor,}

We submit for your consideration the manuscript ``\textbf{Semantic Grounding and Safe Execution for PostGIS Natural-Language-to-SQL},'' a regular research article for \emph{IJGIS}. The present submission is anonymised for double-anonymised peer review; author identity is provided through the editorial system.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Fit to IJGIS.} The manuscript addresses a GIScience question: how to ground natural-language queries so that a large language model can produce executable PostGIS SQL that respects Simple Feature semantics, CRS transformations, the geometry/geography distinction, KNN operators, and safety constraints. Its primary contribution is on the spatial-database side; BIRD is used as a second evaluation track for the warehouse path of the same bilingual framework. The grounding formalisms we encode trace to the $4$- and $9$-intersection topological relations (Egenhofer and Franzosa, 1991; Clementini et al., 1993), the OGC Simple Feature Access standard (\emph{not} a generic LLM benchmark), and the executable semantics of PostGIS.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{What the paper claims, and at what confidence level.}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Robustness as primary claim.} The 40-question Robustness suite is reported as a paired comparison: Full reaches $39/40 = 0.975$ vs.\ baseline $18/40 = 0.450$ (paired McNemar $b=0$, $c=21$, $p<10^{-4}$). Per-category Full accuracy: Security Rejection 11/11, Anti-Illusion 9/9, Schema Enforcement 3/3, Schema Hallucination 8/8, Data Tampering 1/1, OOM Prevention 7/8.

\item \textbf{Spatial reported with explicit variance.} Three independent Full-mode runs at temperature~$0.0$ on the 85-question Spatial set yield mean~$\pm$~SD $0.663 \pm 0.048$; we declare the three-run majority-vote $0.659$ as the primary Spatial headline (paired McNemar $p=0.052$, marginal). Sample 1 ($0.706$, $p=0.003$) and Sample 3 ($0.671$, $p=0.029$) are individually significant; Sample 2 ($0.612$, $p=0.230$) is not.

\item \textbf{BIRD as a clearly secondary track.} BIRD design set $+0.093$ ($p=0.0213$, in-sample tuning); 150q held-out only $+0.033$ ($p=0.3833$, n.s., 95\% CI $[-0.03, 0.09]$). Reported as the current state of the warehouse path, not a confirmed BIRD-wide improvement.

\item \textbf{Cross-lingual gap is not a translation artefact.} Bilingual human re-audit of all 50 LLM-translated BIRD Chinese questions (18~ok, 32~fix, 0~drop); paired re-run of the same pipeline yields paired McNemar $p=1.00$, ruling out translation artefacts.

\item \textbf{Cross-model-family check.} A $2{\times}2$ factorial on the full 85-question GIS Spatial set (Gemini~2.5~Flash vs.\ DeepSeek-V3, $N{=}3$ per cell; Supplement S3) shows schema-only baseline parity between the two families ($p=1.000$) but that the Gemini-tuned grounding stack does not transfer to DeepSeek at the same prompt budget (within-DeepSeek $p=0.597$ null; cross-family full $p=0.008$ in Gemini's favour). We report this honestly as evidence of family-specific grounding portability rather than a universal claim, and scope family-adapted grounding rules as future work.

\item \textbf{External baseline closed.} DIN-SQL on the BIRD 150q held-out set and on the 40-question Robustness suite is reported in Supplement S4: Full $0.507$ vs.\ DIN-SQL $0.440$ on BIRD 150q ($p=0.0755$, marginal), and Full $0.975$ vs.\ DIN-SQL $0.275$ on Robustness 40q ($p=7.45\times10^{-9}$, highly significant).
\end{enumerate}

Mojibake and overfull-hbox layout artefacts are cleaned.
